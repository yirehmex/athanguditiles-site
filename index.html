The craft

A tile that is never fired.

Athangudi tiles take their name from the village they're still made in, a cluster of Chettinad workshops where cement, river sand and mineral oxide pigment are poured onto glass, layer by layer, entirely by hand.

There's no kiln involved. Each tile cures under a bed of sand and water for roughly a week, then is ground and polished by hand until the surface takes on the faint, glassy sheen the glass mould leaves behind. That slow cure is why the colour runs through the tile rather than sitting on top of it — decades of foot traffic wear the pattern thinner, never off.

How each tile is made

Five stages, one glass mould.

The sequence hasn't changed much since the trade began. Skipping a stage, or rushing the cure, is how tiles crack later — so we don't.

01

Mould & outline

A metal stencil sets the pattern's outline directly on a sheet of glass — the glass is what gives the finished face its sheen.

02

Pour the pigment

Cement mixed with mineral oxide is poured into each outlined cell by hand, colour by colour, thinnest layer first.

03

Back with grey cement

A coarser grey cement-and-sand mix backs the tile for strength, then it's pressed under load to bond the layers.

04

Cure under sand

Tiles rest under damp sand for several days — slow curing is what keeps the colour from fading or chalking later.

05

Grind & polish

Once cured, the face is ground level and polished, ready to pack and lay.
